What's Citizens United?
Okay, so let's ignore the fact that your vote on this proposition literally does nothing legally, regardless of whether it passes or fails.

Should you care about Citizens United? Probably ‚Äì it's a hot-button issue. In 2010, the US Supreme Court ruled that spending limits of corporations are unconstitutional because it limits how they can communicate, thus limiting the First Amendment. Spending limits do not apply as long as the contribution is not directly to a candidate, or the political cause that got the donation does not coordinate with the candidate. But that doesn't stop them from directly supporting a candidate through ads, campaigns, etc.

The left would argue that this means groups can contribute unfair amounts of money to politics, making politics more influenced by whoever has money. Ultimately, this did lead to the creation of Super PACs, groups that can spend unlimited amounts of money on politcal campaigns, and do not have to disclose donors. The right would argue that it's actually leveled the playing field, allowing smaller groups to speak up, and the left has benefitted more from this it seems. A lot can be argued on both sides
How are they going to overturn SCOTUS?
It's not specified what exactly legislators would propose should Prop 59 pass, but there are the three ways overturn a SCOTUS ruling:
  • Congress ratifying a Consitutional amendment.
  • At least 38 states approve a Constitutional amendment.[3]
  • SCOTUS overruling itself in a future case.
All are very difficult and unlikely ‚Äì the only time 38 states got together was to repeal Prohibition.[8]

But after 4 years of intense legal battles,[4] it's on the ballot.
